Schnabel, Landon, Ilana M. Horwitz, Peyman Hekmatpour, and Cyrus Schleifer.

DIY Faith.

Contexts (2026).

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

While many young adults are leaving organized religious institutions, they are not abandoning spirituality or belief. Instead, they are practicing their faith in more personal, values-driven ways — a phenomenon the authors call "DIY faith." Drawing on four waves of the National Study of Youth and Religion (2003–2013) and in-depth interviews linked with administrative data, this study follows a cohort of adolescents coming of age during the rapid rise of the "nones." When young people perceive religious institutions as stifling self-actualization, marginalizing sexual minorities, constraining women, or demonstrating hypocrisy, they experience conflict between their religious commitments and deeply held values — and many respond by individualizing their faith rather than abandoning it altogether.

Schnabel, Landon, Ilana Horwitz, Peyman Hekmatpour, and Cyrus Schleifer.

Breaking Free of The Iron Cage: A Bidirectional Theory of Religious Change.

Socius.

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

The United States is undergoing a remarkable religious transformation: in just a few decades, the proportion of religious "nones" surged from 1 in 20 to more than 1 in 4. Through four waves of National Study of Youth and Religion surveys and in-depth interviews (2003–2013) linked with administrative data, this study follows a cohort of adolescents coming of age during the rapid rise of the "nones" and shifting social values. When young people perceive religious institutions as stifling self-actualization, marginalizing sexual minorities, constraining women, or demonstrating hypocrisy, they experience conflict between their religious commitments and deeply held values — and often break free of institutional religion while retaining personal spirituality.

Hekmatpour, Peyman.

World Society, Cultural Diversity, and Gender Gap in Political Empowerment: A Cross-National Comparative Analysis.

Comparative Sociology.

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

This study examines how world society norms and cultural diversity interact to shape the gender gap in political empowerment across nations. Using cross-national comparative data, the analysis finds that integration into world society institutions promotes gender equality in political representation, but this effect is moderated by a country's degree of cultural diversity. The findings contribute to debates about the universality of world polity theory and underscore the importance of accounting for cultural context in cross-national research on gender inequality.

Huft, Justin, and Peyman Hekmatpour.

Income Inequality, World Polity and Political Secularization: A Comparative Historical Analysis.

Sociology of Religion: A Quarterly Review.

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

This study investigates how income inequality and world polity integration jointly shape political secularization across nations over time. Using comparative historical data, the authors find that higher income inequality is associated with slower political secularization, while deeper integration into world polity institutions accelerates the separation of religion from state affairs. The study highlights the dual role of economic structure and global cultural forces in shaping the trajectory of secularization.

Hekmatpour, Peyman.

Foreign direct investment, sectoral distribution and income inequality in developed and developing economies.

International Journal of Sociology 54(3): 133–154.

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

This article examines how the sectoral distribution of foreign direct investment (FDI) shapes income inequality differently in developed versus developing economies. Using panel data across a broad set of countries, the analysis shows that FDI concentrated in extractive and low-skill sectors tends to exacerbate inequality, particularly in developing nations, while FDI in technology and services has more nuanced effects. The findings challenge blanket claims about FDI's equalizing potential and call for greater attention to the compositional character of investment flows.

Mueller, J. Tom, Peyman Hekmatpour, Matthew M. Brooks, and Regina S. Baker.

Further complications to poverty of place: daily poverty dynamics across space.

Population, Space, and Place (online first).

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

This study complicates prevailing understandings of "poverty of place" by examining daily dynamics of poverty across spatial contexts. Moving beyond static, place-based poverty measures, the authors analyze how poverty fluctuates within and across communities at a fine temporal resolution. The findings reveal significant within-place variation that is masked by conventional annual or cross-sectional approaches, with implications for how researchers and policymakers understand the spatial dimensions of economic deprivation.

Vo, Tiffanie, Cyrus Schleifer, and Peyman Hekmatpour.

Asian Americans and Income Inequality: Gendered Disparities between and within Racial and Ethnic Groups.

Sociological Perspectives 66(6): 1103–1124.

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

Using the Current Population Survey, this study investigates income disparity trends across Asian American ethnic groups and gender. While Asian American men and women have relatively high income levels compared to other racial minority groups, these perceived advantages reinforce racial stereotypes and mask significant income variations within these groups and across genders. The findings highlight the dangers of treating Asian Americans as a homogenous group and call for disaggregated analyses that account for social, cultural, and historical diversity within the community.

Jafarigol, Elaheh, William Keely, Tess Hortag, Tom Welborn, Peyman Hekmatpour, and Theodore B. Trafalis.

Religious Affiliation in the Twenty-First Century: A Machine Learning Perspective on the World Value Survey.

Society (online first).

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

This study applies machine learning techniques to the World Values Survey to predict and analyze patterns of religious affiliation across countries in the twenty-first century. Using a range of sociodemographic, economic, and attitudinal predictors, the models identify key factors that distinguish religious from non-religious individuals across diverse national contexts. The findings demonstrate the utility of machine learning for large-scale comparative research on religion and offer new insights into the structural correlates of religious affiliation globally.

Hekmatpour, Peyman, and Carrie M. Leslie.

Ecologically Unequal Exchange and Disparate Death Rates Attributable to Air Pollution: A Comparative Study of 169 Countries from 1991 to 2017.

Environmental Research 212: 113161.

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

This study draws on ecologically unequal exchange (EUE) theory to examine how global economic hierarchies contribute to disparate air pollution mortality rates across 169 countries from 1991 to 2017. The analysis finds that countries in peripheral positions within the world economy bear a disproportionate burden of air pollution deaths, reflecting the environmental costs transferred from core to periphery nations through trade and investment relationships. The findings offer strong cross-national evidence linking global stratification to differential environmental health outcomes.

Hekmatpour, Peyman.

Right-wing stewards: The promoting effect of religiosity on environmental concern among political conservatives in a global context.

Social Problems 69(2): 612–637.

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

Challenging the conventional view that political conservatism suppresses environmental concern, this study finds that religiosity can promote environmental attitudes among conservatives in a global context. Using cross-national survey data, the analysis shows that religious beliefs centered on stewardship of creation motivate environmental concern among right-leaning individuals, particularly in contexts where religious traditions emphasize human responsibility for nature. The findings complicate the assumed relationship between conservatism and environmental skepticism and highlight the role of religious doctrine in shaping ecological values.

Hekmatpour, Peyman.

Secular Values, Economic Development, and Gender Inequality in a Global Context.

Comparative Sociology 20(2): 163–194.

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

This cross-national study examines the joint effects of secular values and economic development on gender inequality. Using structural equation modeling and panel data across a diverse set of countries, the findings show that secular-rational values significantly reduce gender inequality independently of economic development, while the two factors also interact to shape gender outcomes. The results underscore the importance of cultural factors alongside material conditions in explaining global variation in gender equality.

Burns, Thomas J., Tom W. Boyd, and Peyman Hekmatpour.

Elective Affinities in the Anthropocene: Christianity and the Natural Environment Reconsidered.

Social Science, Humanities and Sustainability Research 2(4): 82–95.

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

Drawing on Weber's concept of elective affinities, this study reconsiders the relationship between Christianity and environmental attitudes in the Anthropocene. Rather than treating Christianity as monolithically anti-environmental, the authors identify theological traditions and institutional arrangements within Christianity that foster pro-environmental orientations. The analysis contributes to ongoing debates about religion and ecology, suggesting that certain strands of Christian thought may serve as resources for environmental mobilization in the contemporary era.

Hekmatpour, Peyman.

Inequality and religiosity in a global context: Different secularization paths for developed and developing nations.

International Journal of Sociology 50(4): 286–309.

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

This study examines how the relationship between inequality and religiosity varies between developed and developing nations, challenging one-size-fits-all secularization theories. Using cross-national panel data, the analysis finds that inequality reinforces religiosity in developing countries — where religion serves as a coping mechanism — while showing a weaker or inverse relationship in wealthier nations. The findings call for context-sensitive models of secularization that account for developmental stage and structural inequality.

Hekmatpour, Peyman, and Thomas J. Burns.

Perception of Western governments' hostility to Islam among European Muslims before and after ISIS: the important roles of residential segregation and education.

The British Journal of Sociology 70(5): 2133–2165.

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

This study examines how European Muslims' perceptions of Western governmental hostility toward Islam changed before and after the rise of ISIS, with particular attention to the moderating roles of residential segregation and education. Using longitudinal survey data, the findings show that perceived hostility increased following the ISIS period, but that higher educational attainment and lower residential segregation buffered against this trend. The study sheds light on how structural conditions shape Muslim minority populations' interpretations of state attitudes in the post-9/11 era.

Burns, Thomas J., Peyman Hekmatpour, and Kristen C. Speer.

Human interaction with the natural environment: the POETICAS model as a framework for understanding and praxis in late modernity.

International Journal of Environment and Climate Change 8(3): 234–268.

DOI ↗ Click to view abstract

Abstract

This article presents the POETICAS model as an integrative framework for understanding human interactions with the natural environment in late modernity. The model synthesizes political, organizational, economic, technological, institutional, cultural, aesthetic, and social dimensions of human-environment relations. The authors argue that no single disciplinary lens adequately captures the complexity of environmental problems and that the POETICAS framework offers a more holistic basis for both scholarly analysis and practical intervention in environmental governance.
