Subnational Ecologically Unequal Exchange
& Air Pollution (PM2.5)

A county-level panel analysis of commodity trade networks and ambient fine particulate matter in the United States

This project extends ecologically unequal exchange (EUE) theory to the subnational level, asking whether the dynamics observed between nations — where net exporters of ecologically intensive goods bear disproportionate environmental costs — also operate across US counties. Using the Commodity Flow Survey (CFS) for 2012, 2017, and 2022, the study constructs a directed inter-county shipment network and derives four original measures capturing different dimensions of counties' ecological exchange positions. These are linked to EPA fine particulate matter (PM2.5) data in a county fixed-effects panel model.

Network Measures of Ecological Exchange

A central methodological contribution of this project is the development of four complementary network-based measures that capture distinct dimensions of a county's position within the ecology of commodity exchange. Rather than relying on industry classifications or production-side data alone, these measures are derived directly from observed shipment flows between counties, weighted by shipment value and survey weights.

Ecologically intensive commodities are identified using Standard Classification of Transported Goods (SCTG) codes: minerals and ores (10-14), fossil fuels and petroleum products (15-19), chemicals and hazardous materials (20-24), and primary metals and heavy manufacturing (31-34). All domestic inter-county flows are retained; exports and within-county self-loops are excluded.

1. Net Ecologically Intensive Exchange Position

EUEᵢ = Σ‑WᵢⱼDⱼ − Σ‑WⱼᵢDⱼ
EUEᵢnorm = (Outᵢ − Inᵢ) / (Outᵢ + Inᵢ)

Captures the net balance of ecologically intensive flows. Positive values indicate net senders; negative values indicate net receivers. Normalized to [-1, 1] for cross-county comparability.

2. Eco-Intensive Inflow Dependence

Depᵢ = Σ‑WⱼᵢDⱼ / Σ‑Wⱼᵢ

The share of all incoming flows that are ecologically intensive. Captures the degree to which a county is structurally linked to ecologically intensive activity through what it receives rather than what it produces.

3. Eco-Intensive Production Share

ProdShareᵢ = Σ‑WᵢⱼDⱼ / Σ‑Wᵢⱼ

The share of a county's outgoing flows that are ecologically intensive. Higher values indicate that a larger share of what a county sends out to the rest of the economy is environmentally burdensome production.

4. Transit & Warehousing Share

Medᵢ = Σ‑Wᵢⱼ(48-49) / Σ‑Wᵢⱼ

The share of outgoing flows associated with transportation and warehousing (NAICS 48-49). Identifies counties functioning as logistics intermediaries rather than production sites — a theoretically distinct ecological role.

Together these four measures capture complementary dimensions of ecological exchange: net positional balance, dependence on incoming flows, concentration of outgoing activity, and intermediary network roles. By grounding them in observed shipment networks, the approach avoids imposing assumptions about production or consumption roles and derives ecological positioning directly from patterns of inter-county economic exchange.

Key Findings

The results provide consistent support for extending EUE theory to the subnational level. Net EUE position is positively and significantly associated with PM2.5 across all model specifications, and the effect survives the addition of a comprehensive set of socioeconomic controls and the absorption of all time-invariant county heterogeneity via fixed effects.

The negative coefficient on eco-intensive inflow dependence is theoretically coherent: counties that depend on receiving ecologically intensive goods from elsewhere are externalizing production costs to their trading partners. Counties that depend on inflows rather than local production are, in effect, purchasing an environmental benefit at the expense of the sending counties.

The transit and warehousing share coefficient is also negative and significant, distinguishing the environmental consequences of facilitating exchange from those of driving it. Counties whose economic role is primarily logistical rather than productive bear a markedly lower PM2.5 burden.

Key Results

+Net EUE position predicts higher PM2.5 (β = .23, p < .05, full model)
-Inflow dependence reduces PM2.5 (β = -1.61, p < .001)
+Eco-intensive production share increases PM2.5 (β = .40, p < .05)
-Transit counties have lower PM2.5 (β = -.68, p < .001)
